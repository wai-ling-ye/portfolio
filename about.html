What introduced you to design?

I discovered my interest in design as a gradual process starting with my introduction to photoshop at a young age. I taught myself how to use Photoshop, and at the time I used photoshop to create photo manipulation art. A few years later, I took a digital media class, which expanded my media toolkit to Illustrator and Flash, among other programs. When I entered the University of Rochester, I was majoring in biology, but I always gravitated back to digital media. During my four years, I was always on the executive board of various clubs as the publicity manager. My work with digital media transformed from art to advertising, and I discovered the exhilarating feeling of making art that does work. I loved the feeling of creating posters and ads that not only conveyed a message, but spoke to the emotions and desires of my audience.
When I found out about the field of UX Design, I felt like a fire was lit inside of me. This was a field that focused on function first, and whose goal was to create beautiful things that work beautifully.

Why is design important to you?

Design is like body language. Most of the time, you don’t notice it, but it is a constant and essential aspect of the interaction between a user and a product. Design has in the past created sweeping societal revolutions, most notably with the advent of personal computers, by facilitating communication between a creator and a user. Great design makes complicated ideas and technologies accessible to the wider public, which on the large scale moves us forward as a society, and on the small scale, makes our everyday interactions faster, easier, and more enjoyable.
