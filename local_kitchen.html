Objective

In this new generation of health, environmental, and social consciousness, users need an easier way to cook and eat responsibly. Eating in season produce from local farmers is one way to eat responsibly that consumers can adopt in their daily lives. Local Kitchen makes eating local, in season produce easier than ever by helping you find delicious seasonal recipes and purchase ingredients directly from local farmers.

The Problem

In season produce is healthier, tastier, and better for the environment than produce that is not in season. However, many people do not purchase produce by season because they don’t know which produce is in season, and it’s arduous for most consumers to track what is available locally month to month.

The Solution

Local Kitchen makes it easy for users to eat in season produce by providing them with recipes that will encourage them to buy in season produce to make the recipe. The user never has to think about what produce is in season at all, but simply make recipes they like with the confidence that what they are making a great choice for their health and the environment.

Most user stories led me to prioritize features that we know users want based on the user survey. However, there were two use cases which I wanted to implement features for:

  • A checklist view, especially for mobile, that will allow users to check off grocery list items while shopping in brick and mortar stores

Testing the User Experience

User Testing: Round One

With the initial wireframes completed, I now have enough to perform user testing to determine if the layout and organization of the site make sense to users. I used the Maze app to perform a quick, task-based test with 9 testers. This allows me to see how well users understand the information on the website and how well they are able to navigate through the site based on the layout and copy.

Based on the test results, I found that the majority of users had no problem creating an account, using the search function, viewing recipes, and using checkout. However, there were two unique features I was testing that both turned out to have high failure rates. The first feature is an option for users to view enlarged recipe directions, which would help users see the directions easily while cooking. The second feature is a checklist of ingredients that users will be able to use while grocery shopping. In both instances, users had a hard time identifying the button to use the feature. This is due to a combination of poor labeling, non-descriptive icons, and lack of instruction to the user. Since these are new features that users haven’t already learned to use from other recipe websites, extra steps need to be taken to educate the user on how to use those features.

Because the test results from the wireframe prototype were mostly positive, aside from the two features mentioned above, I went ahead with creating high-fidelity mockups based on the wireframes. I implemented changes to the feature buttons in the high-fidelity mockups to address the issues found in the wireframes.

On the shopping list page, I changed the checklist icon into an icon with accompanying text to clarify the meaning of the link. I also moved the link next to the checkout button and added a print button so that the user can see the link as an additional feature.

On the recipe page, I also changed the “expand” icon into a different icon with accompanying text, again to clarify the meaning. I included a print button here as well to increase usability, and also to hint to the user as to the function of the “Focused View” link.

In addition to the changes to the feature labels, I also made a number of changes based on feedback from my mentor and peers and my own examination of common design patterns.

User Testing: Round Two

After creating the high-fidelity mockups, I performed a second round of user tests. In this round of user testing, I interviewed 4 users as they performed tasks using the high fidelity desktop and mobile prototypes.

In these user tests, I am again testing how well users understand the information on the site and how well they are able to navigate. With the high-fidelity prototype, I am able to examine the effectiveness of the use of color, images, and icons in addition to the layout.

View the testing script

Looking at the issues encountered in the wireframe prototype testing, I found that overall users were more successful in discovering the enlarged recipe directions feature and the grocery checklist feature, but still did not fully immediately grasp the purpose of these features, and likely would not have looked at those features without encouragement.

In order to encourage users to use the recipe directions feature, I implemented a tooltip which would appear automatically for new users or users who are not logged in.

The user testing also revealed a number of other usability issues, most important of which were on the shopping cart page, the search results page, and the profile page.

The confusion on the shopping cart page largely stems from the combination of too many features and a lack of focus on the primary goal of the user. The shopping cart page combined both a shopping cart and the ability to create and save grocery lists.

While at first, I believed that it was logical to put these features together, users actually found that when they are on the checkout page, they expect to see only checkout options, and would expect to see saved grocery lists in the profile section.

I addressed these issues in the next iteration of the mockup by simplifying the shopping cart page and moving all the saved grocery list features to the profile section.

The confusion that users had on the search results page is that the page did not immediately stand out as search results. This is caused by a combination of several factors.

  • The search page is not immediately distinct from the homepage.

  • Users were initially confused to see their favorites at the top of the page, but later said that they would appreciate being able to see their favorite recipes on the search page.

  • The ingredients on the search results page were unexpected because users expect to see only recipes when searching on a recipe website.

I was able to address all three issues on the search results page with a single fix. By moving the ingredients to the top of the page and repurposing it as a search suggestion section, users are able to immediately distinguish the page as a search page and will see only recipes as search results. The elimination of the ingredients from the search results also allows the user to see the general search results immediately below the favorite recipes, which would reconcile the user’s initial confusion of seeing favorite recipes on top.

On the profile page, I initially made the links at the top white to stand out on the dark brown background, but users did not look at or interact with these links at all, and did not recognize them as links. However, these links are in fact important to the user because here they would be able to log out, change their profile image, or access their account settings.

To address this issue, I decided to move all the profile related options into a dropdown menu that users can access by clicking on the profile icon on the toolbar. This also gives another opportunity to bring attention and link to the grocery list feature.

The users who tested the mobile prototype encountered much fewer issues, which were mostly solved by applying the same solution as was used on the desktop mockups.

Creating the Brand Identity

Local Kitchen’s brand identity grew organically through the design, testing, and iterations of the prototype. From the user surveys and competitive analysis performed at the beginning of this project, the website was designed to target health and environmentally conscious consumers.

The site was initially intended to be a place to find recipes using in season ingredients, but I realized early on that seasonal has multiple meanings in relation to food, and this kind of language would need to be explained for users to understand the context.

After writing copy for the wireframes, I considered that using a different focus for the mission statement of the brand might attract more users. To test this, I created a preference test using two different brand names with different brand missions.

The preference test results show that Simple Bites was preferred by two-thirds of the respondents. The brand name “Simple Bites” resonated with users’ desire for simplicity and ease while still being healthy. However, the users who chose Seasonal Eats said they preferred this brand because the message was more specific and meaningful.

I tentatively used the name “Simple Bites” in the high fidelity mockup, but I found through the user testing that this brand name and brand messaging did not communicate the value proposition of the product because it was too vague. I also found through the user testing that, in practice, users liked knowing that they could purchase produce from local farmers through the site, but didn’t know this until they were already going through checkout.

Based on this information, I again shifted the brand differentiator to sourcing produce from local farmers. The beauty of focusing on local farms is that this still accomplishes the original goal of helping users eat in season produce, while at the same time hooking the user’s interest by speaking to their desire to support local farms.

I updated the copy on the homepage to reflect this new theme and changed the brand name to “Local Kitchen”.

After brainstorming using mind maps and sketching, I designed the logo as a combination of a leaf and a map pin, which communicates the brand’s location-driven service and the brand’s focus on fresh ingredients.

The primary colors are a “leafy green” and a “carrot orange”, which combined gives a bright, fresh, and organic feel to the site.

I chose Open Sans for the body text because it is an easy to read and friendly font. I paired this with PT Serif for the logo font and headings because PT Serif shares Open San's open and frienly nature, while also giving a more organic feel.

Finally, I did a third user test with just one user to determine if the fixes I implemented based on the previous user test was effective and to see what impression the new branding will have. This user test revealed that the majority of the fixes I implemented eliminated the major issues encountered in the previous user test.

However, I was surprised to find that the tester did not know the website was a recipe website from looking at the front page. I discussed this with the tester, and they said that they didn’t read the text at the top of the page at all, and there weren’t enough indicators throughout the page to help the user understand that they were looking at recipes.

In my final iteration of Local Kitchen prototype, I updated the copy one last time to be as clear as possible on the purpose and value proposition of the website, and used the word “recipe” as often as possible on the homepage to drive home the message.

Conclusion

Local Kitchen transformed from a seasonal recipes website to an online farmers market that offers recipes using locally grown produce. The user-driven design process played a large part on shaping the brand proposition, as well as allowing us to create attractive, user friendly interfaces, from browsing through checkout.

What I Learned

The biggest lesson I learned from designing Local Kitchen is that website copy is an incredibly important part of the user experience. Throughout the testing phases, many of the major usability issues stemmed from unclear button labelling, or unclear messaging. By improving the copy of a website, we are able to increase users’ trust, understanding, and interest in a brand, on top of improving usability.


The second major lesson I learned is that simplicity is king. It’s important to remember the users’ number one goal of each feature, and to help them achieve that goal as quickly and easily as possible. Extra functionality may seem great on paper, but ultimately may distract and delay users from their goals and lead to abandonment.
